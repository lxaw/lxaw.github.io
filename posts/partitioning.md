> 子路向孔子報告衛君（衛出公）正尋求孔子對施政的意見，並詢問對衛君而言甚麼事最要緊。孔子答：「正名」是最重要的。子路認為孔子太迂腐，孔子反駁道：如果名不正，言論便不通順，做事施政便沒有效果，禮樂、刑罰都會不成樣子，人民會無所適從。

— Confucius, *Analects* (論語), Book XIII, Chapter 3

The [Rectification of Names](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectification_of_names) is, in a distilled version, the idea that disorder comes from a failure to call things by their correct names. 

While this and other teachings of Confucius have later been used as an auspice for government's role, I think that the foundational idea as I see it, is that order itself is the process of taking a continuous stream and partitioning it.

At face value, there are no optimal solutions to any problem. There aren't even problems to be had. There just is. The process of naming, or partitioning, the ambient world is how we can ascribe value and meaning to the objects in our space. There is no underlying reason for anything, and the way that we give reason, is that we first need to partition out the continuous world into discrete chunks that
we then can more easily manipulate. 

I think that naming things, or partitioning the world, is the foundation of what (at least the modern world) views as progress. Without embedding some foundational, measurable (or measurable indirectly by some surrogate) metric to optimize (happiness, wealth, status, etc), there would be no notion of progress, as we would have no definition of a starting point. 

This has similarities to the role of axioms to mathematics. We got to start somewhere, so let's assign a few things as self-evident; only then can we, using these axioms, derive theorems about the world. This is the same underlying idea; only once we ascribe some partitions (i.e, assign some names) to the space we inhabit can we manipulate these partitions to produce new things.

While I do believe fundamentally there is no underlying grandiose function to optimize for the universe, I do believe that happiness stems from being able to partition and assign names. Some people may call this having a work-life-balance (having a strong separation between doing things for pleasure and doing things for an outside metric of money), or others may use this idea for timing gym visits.
It is the basis of self-regulation. In other words, how we divide a stream of time and continuous space into some more discretized outcome.
