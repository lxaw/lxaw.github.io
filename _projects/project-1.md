I am currently working on the THOR (Targeted Hybrid Oncotherapeutic Regulation) project, funded by ARPA-H. Our goal is to develop an implantable device called HAMMR (Hybrid Advanced Molecular Manufacturing Regulator) that could reduce U.S. cancer-related deaths by 50%.
The pinky-sized HAMMR will be surgically implanted and contain genetically engineered cells to attack each patient's cancer cells. My role will be in developing algorithms to compensate for sensor degradation, ensuring accurate monitoring of the patient's cancer and precise dosage adjustments of the therapeutic cells over 6+ months of implantation.

This minimally invasive approach aims to improve immunotherapy outcomes and increase treatment accessibility by reducing burdens and side effects of current standards like chemotherapy.
I collaborate with a multidisciplinary team from Georgia Tech, Rice, UT Austin, Stanford, Carnegie Mellon, Northwestern, Houston, and Johns Hopkins.
